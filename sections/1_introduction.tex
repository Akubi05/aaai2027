\section{Introduction}
Frozen vision-language models (VLMs) provide transferable image--text representations for classification and retrieval \citep{radford2021clip}. Their zero-shot interface leaves systematic failure modes in the representation. Errors can concentrate on demographic groups \citep{agarwal2021evaluating,hall2023gender}, backgrounds, acquisition conditions, pose, or other nuisance factors. Standard average accuracy can conceal these failures.

Most post-hoc VLM debiasing methods start from a known attribute. They encode named concepts in prompts \citep{chuang2023debiasing}, use attribute-labeled reference images \citep{gerych2024bendvlm}, or estimate attribute-predictive subspaces \citep{zhao2026subspace}. This setting covers many audit and mitigation workflows. Other deployments have no reliable group labels: attributes may be unavailable, restricted, incomplete, or mismatched to the factor driving errors. In that regime, task behavior is the main signal. The model reveals which examples it misclassifies and which correct-label margins are small.

We ask whether low margins and prediction errors can identify an editable direction in frozen VLM embedding space. The target is a \emph{latent risk subspace}: low-dimensional within-class variation that predicts calibration failures after class-level semantics are removed and can be suppressed while preserving useful task scores. This definition is operational. A risk direction may track a spurious context, image quality, an intersection of factors, or class evidence. Its effect must be evaluated through held-out group metrics and diagnostics.

\textsc{LatentRisk-VLM} implements this idea as a post-hoc editor for frozen VLM embeddings. Given a task-labeled calibration set, it converts zero-shot margins into risk labels, removes class prototypes and text-aligned evidence, and estimates class-conditional risk directions by residual mean contrast or iterative linear probes. At inference, the true class is unavailable, so each candidate class applies its own correction and produces its own score. The prediction is the largest corrected score across candidates. The primary selection protocol uses task labels only; group attributes are read after prediction to compute audit metrics.

The experiments show a conditional effect. On Waterbirds, rank-1 editing improves worst-group accuracy (WGA) by 26.9 points over frozen CLIP while reducing average accuracy by 5.3 points. Rank-4 editing selected by group-free worst-class validation reaches 63.1 WGA on Waterbirds and 72.8 WGA on CelebA Blond Hair. The same mechanism can also fail: on CelebA, rank-1 editing raises average accuracy from 78.2 to 91.7 while lowering WGA from 71.5 to 54.4. Post-hoc diagnostics explain the contrast. Waterbirds risk scores are enriched for minority-place examples; CelebA risk directions have low alignment with the Male-defined spurious direction. Selector choice creates a second bottleneck: validation-WGA selection raises rank-4 CelebA WGA to 82.2, while fully group-free selectors leave a larger gap.

We make three contributions. First, we formulate class-conditional latent-risk editing for frozen VLMs using task labels and failure signals, with candidate-wise inference that avoids test-label leakage. Second, we give an attribute-label-free selection protocol and measure where it succeeds or fails against group-aware selection. Third, we use attribute-budget, concept-erasure, backbone-transfer, and retrieval audits to characterize the operating regime: failure-derived directions provide useful weak supervision when they align with hidden group structure, and task accuracy alone does not choose robust edits reliably.
