\title{When Do Failures Reveal Spurious Structure?\\A Study of Label-Free Latent-Risk Editing in Frozen Vision-Language Models}
\author{Anonymous Submission}
\affiliations{}

\begin{abstract}
Frozen vision-language models can exhibit concentrated failures across demographic or contextual groups, yet the attributes defining these groups are often unavailable during adaptation. Existing post-hoc debiasing methods typically require named concepts, attribute-labeled references, or group-aware validation, raising the question of whether observable task failures alone can reveal an editable representation structure. We hypothesize that failure-derived directions provide useful weak supervision when they overlap the representation factor defining the hidden worst-performing group. On Waterbirds, task-label-only editing substantially improves worst-group accuracy over frozen CLIP without using place annotations. On CelebA, however, a highly risk-separable rank-1 direction reduces worst-group accuracy, showing that predictable failures may reflect error sources unrelated to the evaluation attribute. Post-hoc diagnostics attribute this contrast to stronger failure–group alignment and minority enrichment on Waterbirds than on CelebA. These results position failure-derived risk editing as conditional weak supervision rather than unsupervised recovery of sensitive attributes: it is useful when failures align with hidden group structure, but risk predictiveness and task-accuracy selection alone are insufficient for group robustness.
\end{abstract}
